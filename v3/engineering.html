Discipline 01 · Core Engineering

Manufacturing Engineering

Preparing and engineering precision sheet metal parts for manufacture — from model to shop floor.

SolidWorksCAD for ManufactureDFM Production EngineeringLaser NestingFabrication

Manufacturing engineering is the discipline everything else rests on. Whether preparing a customer part for production or recreating a model from a supplied drawing, the goal is the same: a manufacturing-ready model that can be cut, bent, welded and assembled right first time, at the lowest sensible cost, on the equipment the factory actually runs.

Sheet Metal CAD for Manufacture · SolidWorks

Preparing customer designs for manufacture — recreating 3D CAD models from customer PDF drawings where STEP files are unavailable, and producing manufacturing-ready models, flat patterns and DXF files for CNC laser cutting and press brake forming.

Design for Manufacture (DFM)

DFM review of parts before they reach the floor — bend radii and deductions, tooling access and clearance, weld preparation, hole and feature placement, and material choice — engineering out the problems that cause scrap and rework.

Production Engineering

Turning drawings into manufacturable jobs — process routings, sequence of operations, work-centre allocation and the work instructions operators follow at each stage of the build.

Nesting & Material Utilisation

Preparing parts for efficient laser nesting on fibre and tube laser — maximising sheet utilisation and minimising offcut and material cost across cutting programmes.

Tooling & Forming

Press brake tooling selection and bend sequencing, and practical fixturing and jig thinking for welding and assembly — so parts form and locate consistently.

Welding, Fabrication & Assembly

Engineering support across welding, fabrication and assembly — weldment detailing, fit-up and build sequence — bridging the gap between the drawing and what happens at the bench.

Why it matters

A part that is prepared and engineered well never becomes a production problem. Sound DFM, correct routings and clear work instructions are the quiet, upstream engineering that keeps a precision sheet metal shop flowing — and the reason I can see, from the model stage, where a job will slow down on the floor.

Discipline 02 · Process & Quality

Process Engineering

Standardising, improving and controlling how the factory works — SOPs, ISO 9001 & 14001 support, traceability and continuous improvement.

SOP DevelopmentProcess ImprovementISO 9001 & 14001 TraceabilityContinuous ImprovementManufacturing Support

Process engineering is about the system of work, not the individual part — making the right way to do a job the standard way, then measuring it, controlling it and improving it. It is the discipline that keeps quality repeatable across operators and shifts, and it is where the case for digitising the floor first became obvious.

SOP Development

Writing standard operating procedures and work instructions that capture the correct method for a process — so the same job is done the same way regardless of who is on the machine, and new operators come up to standard faster.

Process Improvement

Reviewing how work actually flows through the shop, finding the bottlenecks, rework loops and wasted movement, and re-engineering the process to remove them — practical, floor-level lean thinking.

Traceability

Establishing traceability of parts, jobs and labour through production — who worked on what, when, and against which work order — so quality issues can be traced to source and accountability is built in, not reconstructed.

ISO 9001 & 14001 Support

Contributing to ISO 9001 and ISO 14001 implementation — developing SOPs, producing engineering and organisational documentation, process standardisation and document and revision control.

Manufacturing Support

Day-to-day engineering support for the floor — resolving production and documentation queries, handling engineering changes, and keeping jobs moving when reality diverges from the drawing.

Continuous Improvement

Treating improvement as ongoing rather than a project — each SOP, traceability step and process change compounds, and the digital systems became the most effective continuous-improvement tool of all.

Why it matters

Standardised, controlled, traceable processes are what let a precision manufacturer scale quality without adding headcount. Every SOP written and every process improved was a lesson in exactly where paper, manual filing and end-of-shift data entry were holding the factory back — which is precisely what the digital transformation programme set out to fix.
